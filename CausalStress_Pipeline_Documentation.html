Executive Summary

CausalStress is a financial stress-testing system that replaces correlation-based scenario generation with a causally grounded, regime-conditional framework. It ingests 20 years of macroeconomic and market data across 56 variables, detects the prevailing market regime in real time, and generates portfolio stress scenarios through a Vector Autoregression model conditioned on historically stressed periods — with shocks propagated through a learned causal graph rather than assumed to move independently.

The system was validated on 7 held-out crisis events spanning 2016 to 2023 — events not used in any modelling or configuration decision. It achieved 90.0% out-of-sample scenario coverage, 77.6% directional accuracy, and 100% pairwise consistency across the 6 key financial variables. These results are statistically significant against standard-practice baselines (p=0.008 vs Historical Replay, p=0.031 vs Unconditional VAR). A planned future extension will formally compare causal model projections against DFAST regulatory scenarios across multiple historical cycles to assess systematic divergence.

1. Product Vision & Strategic Positioning

1.1 Vision Statement

Financial stress testing has historically relied on correlation — the assumption that variables which moved together in the past will move together in the future. This assumption holds in calm markets. It breaks down precisely when it matters most: during crises, when contagion activates transmission channels that are dormant under normal conditions.

CausalStress is built on a single core proposition: stress scenarios should reflect how financial crises actually propagate, not how variables happened to correlate over a 20-year average. The system learns directed causal relationships from data, identifies 211 contagion edges that activate only during market stress, and uses those edges to initialise and propagate shocks through a regime-conditioned generative model.

1.2 Market Opportunity

Bank stress testing is a regulatory requirement, not a discretionary activity. In the United States, the Dodd-Frank Act Stress Testing (DFAST) framework requires banks with assets above $100 billion to demonstrate capital adequacy under severely adverse macroeconomic scenarios annually. Equivalent frameworks operate in the European Union (EBA stress tests), the United Kingdom (Bank of England CBES), and internationally under Basel III/IV Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) requirements.

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis demonstrated the practical cost of correlation-based stress testing. Banks and regulators using standard models did not anticipate the speed or severity of cross-asset contagion once the credit market began to seize — because their models were calibrated on calm-period correlation and did not capture the 211 contagion edges that activated during the crisis. CausalStress was built to close that specific gap.

The system also addresses a secondary need: real-time regime monitoring. The current market (as of the latest data) has been in an Elevated regime for 275+ consecutive trading days, a fact that directly informs portfolio positioning and hedging decisions but is invisible to models that do not explicitly detect market states.

1.3 Core Value Proposition

Causation, not correlation. The causal discovery engine (DYNOTEARS + PCMCI ensemble) recovers all 25 known ground-truth economic causal relationships with 100% recall at 15× the precision of random baseline. The resulting causal graph encodes which variables drive which — not merely which variables co-move.

Regime conditioning. The scenario generator trains its VAR model exclusively on 3,105 stressed-regime days — elevated, stressed, high-stress, and crisis periods — rather than the full 20-year average. Scenarios therefore reflect crisis dynamics, not the diluted signal of mixing calm and stressed periods.

Statistically validated fat tails. Student-t innovations with data-fit degrees of freedom (df=5.97 in normal regimes, df=3.84 in crisis) outperform Gaussian innovations on a KS goodness-of-fit test for all 20 variables tested in both regimes. This matters for tail risk: at df=3.84, extreme daily moves are approximately three times more likely than a Gaussian model would predict.

Independently validated performance. The 90% out-of-sample coverage figure is computed on 7 events (2016–2023) that were never used in any model selection or configuration decision. The statistical significance against standard-practice baselines (Historical Replay p=0.008, Unconditional VAR p=0.031) was computed via Wilcoxon signed-rank test on paired per-event coverage scores.

2. Target Customers & Use Cases

2.1 Ideal Customer Profile

Bank risk management teams responsible for DFAST or ICAAP submissions. These teams need to generate internally consistent stress scenarios across multiple asset classes, demonstrate that scenarios are severe but plausible to regulators, and explain why their projections differ from the regulator's baseline. CausalStress directly supports all three: it generates 400 weighted scenario paths per shock type, scores each for financial plausibility, and provides a causal explanation of how each scenario propagates through the portfolio.

Asset managers and hedge funds running multi-asset portfolios who need stress testing beyond standard VaR. The system supports 16 portfolio assets across equities, fixed income, commodities, and currencies, with results decomposed by sector contribution and marginal risk.

Insurance companies subject to Solvency II or equivalent stress requirements who need scenario generation across correlated asset classes over multi-quarter horizons.

Academic and research teams studying systemic risk, financial contagion, and causal inference in economic time series. The full pipeline — data ingestion, causal discovery, regime detection, scenario generation — is implemented end-to-end and independently validated against historical crises.

2.2 Priority Use Cases

DFAST scenario benchmarking (planned). The system includes infrastructure to load official DFAST scenario CSVs and compare them against causal model projections. A formal backtested comparison across multiple DFAST cycles is planned as a future extension — see Future Scope section.

Portfolio stress testing across multiple crisis families. Six shock families (market crash, credit crisis, rate shock, global shock, volatility shock, pandemic/exogenous) each generate 400 weighted scenario paths. The stress test decomposes portfolio loss by asset, computes VaR at 95% and 99%, CVaR, and maximum drawdown, and identifies the assets contributing most to tail risk.

Causal contagion analysis. The causal graph explorer shows which variables drive which, filtered by regime. The 211 stress-only edges — contagion channels dormant in calm markets — are highlighted directly, allowing risk managers to identify which portfolio exposures are most vulnerable to regime-conditional amplification.

Real-time regime monitoring. The system classifies the current market state daily across 6 regimes (calm through crisis) with confidence probabilities and a transition matrix. As of the latest data, the market has been in an Elevated regime for 275+ consecutive trading days — a persistent pre-stress state that informs position sizing and hedging decisions.

Stage 2 — Data Processing

data_processor.py executes 6 steps in sequence:

  1. Load — Reads both raw tables. FRED: 6,592 date×series rows. Yahoo: 5,582 date×ticker rows.
  2. Align — Yahoo trading calendar becomes the master. FRED's monthly/quarterly data is forward-filled to daily — each trading day carries the most recent available macro reading.
  3. Impute — 50.97% of the aligned dataset is missing. Small gaps are interpolated, larger gaps are forward-filled. After imputation: 0% missing.
  4. Stationarity transforms — Prices → log-returns log(today/yesterday). Rates/spreads → first-difference today − yesterday. Level indicators → none (already stationary).
  5. Engineer 10 features — 21-day and 63-day rolling volatility for S&P/Nasdaq/Russell/VIX, VIX z-score, HY spread z-score, credit spread gap, distress spread, SOFR-CP spread proxy.
  6. Store — 368,346 records (5,581 days × 66 variables) in batches of 10,000 into processed.time_series_data.

Stage 3 — Regime Detection

hmm_model.py (using GaussianMixture as replacement for hmmlearn):

  1. Load 8 regime features from processed data: VIX, HY spread, yield curve, S&P 500 returns, MOVE index, TED spread, St. Louis FSI, 21-day realized vol.
  2. Standardize to mean=0, std=1.
  3. BIC selection — Fit GMM for 2–6 states. Lower BIC wins. Result: 6 states.
  4. Fit final GMM — 6 Gaussians in 8-dimensional space, 5 random restarts.
  5. Assign each of 5,561 days to its most likely Gaussian cluster.
  6. Sort by VIX mean and label: calm → normal → elevated → stressed → high_stress → crisis.
  7. Compute transition matrix empirically from the label sequence.
  8. Validate against 15 historical crises → 93% accuracy.
  9. Store 5,561 regime rows into models.regimes.

Stage 4 — Causal Discovery (Pre-computed)

Two algorithms run in ensemble on a capable machine (outputs stored as ensemble_causal_graph.json and regime_causal_graphs.json, loaded into the database):

  • DYNOTEARS: Treats causal discovery as a constrained LASSO regression. Finds which variable X at time t predicts variable Y at times t and t+1. Outputs edge weights.
  • PCMCI: Tests conditional independence. "Does X cause Y after controlling for everything else?" Outputs p-values per edge.

Ensemble rule: Only keep edges found by BOTH algorithms = 255 consensus edges. Run separately per regime → 6 regime-specific graphs. All stored in models.causal_graphs.

Stage 5 — Scenario Generation

scenario_generator.py defines 6 shock families × 400 scenarios each = 2,400 total paths (4 generated in the current run; 2 defined and ready). For each shock type:

  1. Load training data — Join processed data + regime labels. Filter to elevated/stressed/high_stress/crisis days → 3,105 observations.
  2. Select 25 variables — 20 core variables + shock variable + highest-variance remaining.
  3. Fit VAR(lag=5) — Ridge regression on 3,105 stressed-regime days. Compute two covariance matrices: normal residuals and worst-10%-of-days (crisis covariance).
  4. Define multi-root shock template — Multiple variables shocked simultaneously. Templates derived empirically from actual crisis window data (see shock families table below).
  5. 25 variables selected for VAR — see table below.
  6. Causal propagation — Apply shock through causal graph up to 3 hops, 40% decay per hop.
  7. Generate 400 paths × 60 days — Multi-shock-level schedule. Student-t noise (df=5.97 normal, df=3.84 crisis). ±6σ daily clip. 5-day shock persistence (×0.72 decay per day).
  8. Score plausibility — Financial consistency checks. Mean: 0.75–0.80.
  9. Soft-filter and store — All 400 paths kept but weighted by plausibility. Stored as JSONB in models.scenarios.

PART II — Why Each Stage Exists

The reasoning for each step in simplest terms, with validated proof tables and values wherever necessary.

Stage 1 — Why Collect This Specific Data?

The question being answered

What data actually predicts financial crises before they happen?

Why FRED (macro) + Yahoo (market) together?

Markets move on two timescales. Market prices (VIX, S&P 500, credit spreads) react within minutes. Macro data (unemployment, GDP, bank lending) moves over months. A stress-testing system that only watches prices misses the slow-building vulnerabilities — a yield curve inversion 12 months before a recession, bank lending standards tightening 6 months before a credit crunch.

Using both means the model can see:

  • Leading signals (yield curve, bank lending standards, consumer sentiment) — warn before the crash
  • Coincident signals (VIX, credit spreads, S&P returns) — confirm the crash is happening
  • Cross-market transmission (how oil prices cause credit spread widening, how rate shocks hit equities through the financial sector)

Why these 35 FRED series specifically?

Each series covers a distinct channel through which financial stress originates or propagates. No two series are redundant — each captures a mechanism the others don't:

Why these 21 Yahoo tickers specifically?

The 21 tickers cover every major asset class that moves during a financial crisis — equities by broad index and by sector, volatility across equities and bonds, commodities, currencies, and fixed income across the credit quality spectrum. The sector ETF breakdown (XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLY, XLRE, XLU) matters specifically because sector rotation is the primary mechanism through which equity stress propagates — a credit crisis hits XLF first, a rate shock hits XLRE and XLU first, an energy shock hits XLE first. Without sector-level data, the causal graph cannot distinguish these transmission paths.

Stage 2 — Why Process Data This Way?

The question being answered

How do you make 35 macro series and 21 market series comparable and usable by a statistical model?

Why forward-fill FRED to daily?

FRED releases GDP quarterly and unemployment monthly. But markets move every day. Without forward-filling, 95% of the dataset would be blank for macro variables. Forward-filling says "the latest GDP reading is still valid until the next one comes out" — which is literally true, since that's the number markets are pricing from.

Why stationarity transforms?

The VAR model (used in Stage 5) assumes the data has constant statistical properties over time. S&P 500 at 5,000 in 2024 vs 1,000 in 2008 — the levels are incomparable. But the daily return (how much it moved today) is comparable across decades. Without this transform, the model would confuse "S&P was at a higher level in 2024" with "S&P was more volatile in 2024."

  • Log-returns for prices: log(5001/5000) = +0.02% — symmetric, scale-invariant
  • First-difference for rates: 4.5% − 4.4% = +10bps — measures the change in interest rate level

Why engineer features?

Raw variables capture levels and changes. But regime detection also needs to see volatility clustering (are we in a high-vol period?) and z-scores (how extreme is VIX right now relative to its history?). These 10 engineered features encode that context directly.

Stage 3 — Why Regime Detection? Why GMM?

The question being answered

Why can't we just run one model on all the data?

Why regimes at all?

Financial markets don't behave the same in all conditions. The relationship between oil prices and equities is completely different in a crisis vs a calm period. In calm markets, oil rising slightly = positive (growth signal). In a crisis, oil crashing = risk-off contagion. If you train one VAR on 20 years of mixed data, you average across these two completely different behaviours and get a model that's wrong in both.

Regime conditioning lets the scenario generator ask: "Given that we're in a stressed regime, how does a rate shock propagate?" rather than averaging across 20 years of mixed market conditions.

Why GMM over HMM?

HMM was designed for problems where the state is truly hidden — speech recognition, DNA sequencing. But market regimes aren't hidden. VIX=52 is directly observable. The stress is visible in the data. GMM exploits this directly by clustering each day's observable features without needing temporal inference.

Stage 4 — Why Causal Discovery? Why Two Algorithms?

The question being answered

Which variables actually cause which — and does that causal structure change under stress?

Why causal discovery at all, not just correlation?

Standard stress tests use correlation. If S&P and credit spreads are correlated, they move together — but correlation doesn't tell you why or which direction. In 2008, did credit spreads cause equities to fall, or did equity falls cause credit spreads to widen? The direction matters enormously for a stress test — it determines what you shock first and what follows.

Causal discovery finds directed relationships: XLF → HYG means the financial sector causes high-yield bonds to move, not the reverse. This lets the scenario generator propagate shocks in the right direction through the economy.

Why two algorithms (DYNOTEARS + PCMCI)?

Each has different blind spots. DYNOTEARS uses LASSO regression — fast, finds strong linear relationships, but can confuse correlation with causation. PCMCI uses conditional independence tests — statistically rigorous, controls for confounders, but slower and can miss weak relationships.

Ensemble rule: Only keep edges that BOTH algorithms find. This aggressively controls false positives — if one method finds an edge but the other doesn't, it's probably noise.

Stage 5 — Why This Scenario Generation Approach?

The question being answered

How do you generate realistic financial crisis scenarios that are both extreme enough and financially consistent?

Why these specific 25 variables for the VAR?

The 25 were chosen to represent every major asset class present in a diversified institutional portfolio, while keeping the VAR tractable. With 25 variables and lag-5, the VAR has 25 × (25×5 + 1) = 3,150 coefficients to estimate — requiring the 3,105 stressed-regime days to be statistically identified (roughly 1:1 observations-to-parameters ratio, regularised by ridge regression). Adding more variables would increase estimation error; the 25 selected cover the full cross-asset stress surface with minimal redundancy. The 6 key backtest variables (^GSPC, ^VIX, DGS10, CL=F, XLF, BAMLH0A0HYM2) are all within the 25 — so the validation metrics directly measure how well the system generates the variables that matter most for portfolio stress.

Why train the VAR only on stressed-regime days?

If you train on all 5,581 days, the model learns average behaviour — mostly calm, occasionally stressed. The resulting scenarios would be too mild because they average 70% calm days with 30% stressed days. Training only on the 3,105 elevated/stressed/high_stress/crisis days means the VAR learns how variables move when markets are already under pressure — which is exactly what a stress test needs.

Why multi-root shocks (5 variables simultaneously)?

Real crises don't start with one variable moving. The 2008 GFC wasn't "credit spreads widened." It was simultaneously: equity crash + VIX explosion + financial sector collapse + credit freeze + flight to treasuries. Shocking only one variable produces scenarios that are historically unrealistic — they miss the correlated multi-asset selloff that actually defines a crisis.

Why Student-t innovations instead of Gaussian?

Financial returns have fat tails — extreme events happen far more often than a normal distribution predicts. On a Gaussian assumption, a 5-sigma move should happen once every 3.5 million trading days. In practice it happens every few years.

Why causal propagation through the graph?

A pure VAR shock says "S&P falls 3σ today." But in reality an S&P fall immediately triggers: XLF falls (financial sector exposed), credit spreads widen (risk-off), VIX spikes (vol demand), TLT rallies (flight to safety). Without propagating through the causal graph, the scenario misses all these simultaneous co-movements that happen within minutes.

The ±6σ clip and 5-day persistence — why?

Real crises sustain extreme moves for days. The 2008 GFC had S&P falling 5σ+ on multiple consecutive days, not just one. Without persistence, the shock disappears after day 1 and the scenario looks like a one-day blip. The 0.72 daily decay means by day 5 the initial shock is at 0.72^5 = 19% of its original size — fading realistically rather than vanishing.

The ±6σ clip prevents numerical blow-up while allowing realistic extreme moves (real crises do hit 5–6σ daily moves).

Why 400 paths per shock type? Why 6 families?

Statistical convergence. You need enough paths to reliably estimate the 5th and 95th percentiles of the outcome distribution. With fewer paths, the tail estimates jump around. 400 paths gives stable quantile estimates for 6 key variables over 60 days.

The 6 families cover the four distinct transmission mechanisms through which financial crises propagate — equity-led, credit-led, rates-led, commodity-led — plus two special cases (pure volatility spike, and simultaneous multi-system shock). A portfolio that survives all 6 families is stress-tested across the full observable space of crises since 2000. The families were derived inductively from crisis_library.py, which catalogues every major crisis since 2000, groups it by primary transmission mechanism, and extracts actual σ-magnitudes from real processed data during each crisis window. Guardrails were then set at the observed historical extremes to prevent the generator from producing physically impossible scenarios.

Future Scope

DFAST Regulatory Scenario Integration

The pipeline includes infrastructure to load official DFAST Severely Adverse scenario CSVs into the regulatory.scenarios table and compare them against causal model projections variable-by-variable across 13 quarters. An initial exploratory comparison against the DFAST 2026 scenario was run and logged in the system — projections diverged significantly on credit and rate variables — but this has not been formally backtested against realised DFAST outcomes.

Planned work: Formalise the comparison methodology, define a rigorous divergence metric, backtest against multiple historical DFAST cycles (2019–2026), and produce a validated quantitative assessment of where causal projections and regulatory projections systematically differ and why. This would provide a data-grounded basis for supplementing or challenging regulatory scenario assumptions.

Reinforcement Learning Adversarial Engine

A separate RL-based adversarial stress-testing engine is implemented in ml_pipeline/generative_engine_rl/ and partially trained, but not yet integrated into the main pipeline. Its purpose and positioning differ from the generative scenario engine:

Generative Engine (VAR)Adversarial Engine (RL)
Question answeredWhat range of outcomes is plausible under this shock?What shock sequence maximises loss for this specific portfolio?
ApproachStochastic simulation — 400 weighted pathsNeural bandit / PPO search over action space
OutputDistribution of portfolio outcomesSingle worst-case shock pathway per portfolio profile
Use caseRegulatory scenario coverage, VaR, CVaRAdversarial stress testing, concentrated risk discovery

The RL engine frames stress testing as a sequential decision problem: at each step, an agent selects which variable to shock and by how much, with the goal of maximising portfolio loss over a multi-step horizon while maintaining causal fidelity (shocks must follow the causal graph). Four portfolio profiles are supported — balanced, tech-heavy, bond-heavy, credit-heavy — with separate trained policies per profile, because the worst-case shock pathway differs fundamentally by portfolio composition.

Planned work: Complete training across all profiles and shock magnitudes, integrate the adversarial endpoint into the frontend stress-test UI, and validate adversarial pathways against historical worst-case episodes (Oct 2008, Mar 2020) to confirm the RL agent discovers historically grounded shock sequences rather than arbitrary worst-case combinations.
